Innovating Learning and Education in the era of AI

Artificial Intelligence is transforming and disrupting many industries and markets. Around the world, governments and companies are proclaiming we are entering the “era of AI” with the rapid development of intelligent devices that can recognize faces and interact via speech, robots that can work alongside people to help automate warehouse logistics and manufacture goods, algorithms that can generate novel photo realistic images and music, computers that can provide decision support to clinicians to help detect cancer more reliably, and so much more.

As computers continue to automate more and more routine tasks, inequity of education remains a key barrier to future opportunities where success depends increasingly on intellect, creativity, empathy, and having the right skills and knowledge. This accelerating change raises the critical question of how to best prepare students, from children to life-long learners, to be successful and to flourish in the era of AI.



AI Literacy in K-12

As computational thinking has become recognized as a new literacy for the 21st century, AI literacy is becoming recognized as important for STEM education on a global scale. We are actively working with schools, afterschool programs, education non-profits, and industry collaborators to develop a comprehensive K-12 AI education program to serve students, teachers, and families both nationally and worldwide.

We are extending student-friendly coding platforms (such as Scratch, App Inventor, and Jupyter Notebooks) with AI services, content, and curriculum to empower students to learn via playful experimentation, creative expression, teamwork, critical thinking and problem solving. We are also developing teacher training materials, project guides, assessments and standards aligned with the AAAI-CTSA’s Big 5 Ideas of AI: machine perception, knowledge representation and decision making, machine learning, human-AI interaction and societal impact of AI. We are also integrating ethical design concepts and practices so that students appreciate issues of bias, fairness, transparency, and privacy in what they create.



AI for Vocational-Technical and Adult Education.

Vocational-technical students and adult learners need to understand and apply AI concepts/practices so they can engage in rewarding professional opportunities in the growing AI-economy. One goal is to create new AI Career Pathways for vocational-technical schools. Another goal is to drive innovation towards continuous, agile adult education programs in AI.

We shall pair collaborative online learning experiences with in-person Action Learning Labs to enable voc-tech and post-secondary schools to offer new, engaging, and effective approach to learning and training. We are collaborating with industry, voc-tech schools, and our teacher training programs to create curriculums, teacher-training materials, and assessments that keep pace with this rapidly evolving area. We also see opportunity to develop AI tutors to amplify teacher training programs to scale adult capacity building cost-effectively. Our ultimate goal is to drive transformative impact that provides employment opportunities to close the prosperity & opportunity divide -- starting in Massachusetts and then expanding across America’s Heartland and around the globe.



AI and Personalized Learning in the Classroom

There is tremendous opportunity to incorporate AI into the classroom in a personalized and emotionally compelling way that is effective, scalable and affordable. Such technologies should empower teachers and engage students while respecting privacy and being mindful of broader social implications.

In particular, intelligent agents that serve as leaning companions, mentors, or practice partners have the potential to supplement and assist teachers – especially in much needed areas such as special needs education or for children who need additional support. We aspire to develop AI-enabled solutions to address problems that challenge our nation’s ability to provide high quality education to all children. For instance, this includes supporting children who lack access to affordable quality preschool programs, are English language learners, have learning disabilities, or have developmental disorders such as ASD. In addition, we believe that developing new tools to support a continuity of intervention for parents at home, the education team at school, and clinical teams as needed will yield significant positive results.



Inclusivity and Creativity in the Era of AI

Developing high-tech solutions to complex problems requires creativity, social intelligence, critical thinking, ethical judgement, and working as part of a team with diverse perspectives and expertise. Creative activities coupled with acting and designing with compassion towards others brings meaning, purpose and joy to people’s lives. Cultivating these qualities in the future workforce is not just an economic imperative, it is also critical for human fulfillment and opportunity as people work closely and collaboratively with intelligent machines and each other. By democratizing AI through education, training, and outreach activiites in collaboration with non-profit organizations, we aim to inspire and empower a far more diverse and inclusive group of citizens to apply AI technologies to improve their lives and solve problems facing their communities. We are working with organizations who serve under-represented communities.This includes hosting expositions and competitions for K-12 students as a way to showcase their AI for Social Good projects to grow a vibrant community of young AI makers and innovators.

Inclusivity in AI

  • DoodleBot and Creative AI

    Personal Robots Group

    We are developing a kit for high school students to build and train a robot to draw doodles to learn about concepts in generative/creative AI.

  • Scratch and Creative Learning

    Lifelong Kindergarten

    Scratch helps young people learn to think creatively, reason systematically, and work collaboratively — essential skills for life in the 21st century.

  • PopBots for Early AI Education

    Personal Robots Group

    PopBots are programmable, intelligent social robots that young children can build, program, train and play with to learn about AI concepts in an intuitive way.

  • GANPaint extension in Scratch

    MIT Quest, Bridge

    Kids can learn about Generative Adversarial Networks by using GANPaint (CSAIL) in Scratch to draw scenes with object-level control using a deep network.

  • GenAI for Primary School Classrooms

    Personal Robots Group

    GenAI is a project-based curriculum with a robot kit that introduces elementary school students to robotics and artificial intelligence.

  • Jupyter Notebooks for High School AI

    MIT Quest, Bridge

    We are developing a series of Jupyter notebooks for high school students to learn about a range of AI concepts, methods and practices such as GANs.

  • PopPet: A ScratchX Compatible Robot

    Personal Robots Group

    PopPet is a simple Arduino robot that elementary school children can customize and program using the GenAI project-based curriculum and a chromebook.

  • Social Robots for Early Literacy

    Personal Robots Group

    Social robots play educational games with young children to provide personalized learning experiences that improve children's vocabulary and language skills.

  • Improving Growth Mindset with Social Robots

    Personal Robots Group

    We are investigating if playing challening games with a social robot can promote a growth mindset in young children to foster curiosity and other achievements.

  • AI + Ethics Curriculum for Middle School

    Personal Robots Group

    A modular, project-based AI and Ethics curriculum to empower students to be conscientious consumers and designers of AI technologies.

  • Designing Conversational Agents

    MIT App Inventor

    Conversational Agents is a block-based coding tool that simplifies mobile app development.

  • The Personal Image Classifier

    MIT App Inventor

    The PIC tool is an intuitive web interface where users can train and test their own image classifiers and use them on MIT App Inventor to create mobile apps.

  • Zhorai: Teaching a Conversational Agent

    MAS.s65/6.s898 Final Project

    A class project from "Democratizing AI through K-12 Education" of Spring 2019 to teach upper elementary students about natural langauge interaction with chatbots.

  • Beaver Works Summer Institute at MIT

    MIT Lincoln Laboratory

    This is a world-class STEM program for talented high school students that offers opportunities to learn about AI and other technologies through hands-on projects.

Cynthia Breazeal

Associate Professor, MIT Media Lab, Personal Robots Group ● Associate Director of Strategic Initiatives for the Bridge, MIT Quest for Intelligence


Breazeal leads this project with Sanjay Sarma to democratize AI and foster diversity and inclusivity in an AI literate society.

Her research investigates new ways for K-12 students to learn about AI concepts, practices and ethics by designing, programming, training and interacting with robots, AI toolkits and other technologies. Her research also develops personalized learning companions for language, literacy and social-emotional development.

Sanjay Sarma

Professor, Department of Mechanical Engineering ● Vice President, MIT Open Learning Initiative


Sarma is the VP for Open Learning at MIT, which includes the Office of Digital Learning, the MIT Integrated Learning Initiative

and the Abdul Latif Jameel World Education Lab. His research developed new standards for RFID and he has been very influental in the field of Internet of Things -- that are becoming increasingy intelligent and connected. His core interest areas for this project are K-12 AI education, vocational-technical AI training, and the future workforce.


Hal Abelson

Professor of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science, MIT CSAIL ● Founder, App Inventor


Abelson is a pioneer of constructionist learning and computational thinking.

He has written numerous textbooks and developed high impact tools and programming languages to help students of all ages learn about AI and computer science. His group developed App Inventor that empowers people with little programming experience to write mobile phone applications. For this project, his team is expanding App Inventor to help students learn about AI and develop AI-enabled mobile apps.

Abby Everett Jaques

Postdoctoral Assoiate, Department of Philosophy ● Ethics Project Lead, MIT Quest for Intelligence


Jaques's research is centered in moral and political philosophy and the philosophy of action.

She is particularly interested in the relationship of ethics, AI and society. She is co-organizer of MIT's Computational Cultures initiative, and she is a Research Fellow in Digital Ethics at the Jain Family Institute. She leads several curriculum projects aimed at reinventing how we teach ethics to engineers. For this project, she has been extending these frameworks to K12 AI Ethics. She recently joined Stanford University.

John Gabrieli

Professor, Department of Brain and Cognitive Sciences ● Director, Martinos Imaging Center

He also has appointments at the Harvard Graduate School of Education and the Department of Psychiatry at Massachusetts General Hospital. His research focus is on the neural mechanisms of memory, cognition, nad emotion in the human brain -- and how those mechanisms are disrupted in neurological and psychiatric disorders.
