A New MIT Project

Innovating Learning and Education in the era of AI

Artificial Intelligence is transforming and disrupting many industries and markets. Around the world, governments and companies are proclaiming we are entering the “era of AI” with the rapid development of intelligent devices that can recognize faces and interact via speech, robots that can work alongside people to help automate warehouse logistics and manufacture goods, algorithms that can generate novel photo realistic images and music, computers that can provide decision support to clinicians to help detect cancer more reliably, and so much more.

As computers continue to automate more and more routine tasks, inequity of education remains a key barrier to future opportunities where success depends increasingly on intellect, creativity, empathy, and having the right skills and knowledge. This accelerating change raises the critical question of how to best prepare students, from children to life-long learners, to be successful and to flourish in the era of AI.



AI Literacy in K-12

Just as computational thinking has become recognized as a new literacy for the 21st century, AI literacy is rapidly becoming recognized as important for STEM education on a global scale. We are actively working with schools, afterschool programs, education non-profits, and industry collaborators to develop the first comprehensive K-12 AI education program to serve students, teachers, and families nationally and worldwide. We believe that the best way to learn about AI-based concepts and practices is through hands-on projects supported by peers and mentors. We are extending student-friendly coding platforms (such as Scratch, App Inventor and Jupityr Notebooks) with AI services, content, and curriculum to empower students to create personally and socially meaningful projects in a spirit of playful experimentation, creative expression, teamwork, critical thinking and problem solving. We are engaging in an iterative, evidence-based, participatory design process to develop accompanying mentor and teacher training materials, project guides and assessments. Our curriculum and projects are aligned with the emerging AAAI-CTSA’s Big 5 Ideas of AI: machine perception, knowledge representation and decision making, machine learning, human-AI interaction and social/ethical implications of AI. Though our innovative approach, students learn how intelligent machines think and learn through designing, programming, training, and interacting with them. We are also integrating ethical design concepts and practices throughout our curriculum and activities so that students appreciate issues of bias, fairness, transparency, and privacy in their own projects. MIT will also host expositions and competitions for K-12 students as a way to showcase and share their AI projects for social good and grow a vibrant community of young AI makers and innovators.



AI Career Pathway for Vocational-Technical Education.

In response to how AI is transforming the future of work, we aim to prepare students in vocational-technical training to learn, understand, and apply AI concepts and practices so they can engage in rewarding professional opportunities in the growing AI-economy. Expanding upon our K-12 AI efforts, we are developing a hands-on approach to vocational-technical training -- where students participate in a creative process of learning-through-making with modern AI tools and cognitive services while collaborating and sharing with peers on projects of personal passion and meaning. Our goal is to create an AI Career Pathway for vocational-technical schools, starting in Massachusetts.
This pathway curriculum includes core AI literacy concepts, tools, and skills paired with Action Learning Labs to enable vocational-technical schools to offer a truly innovative approach to learning. In doing so, we aspire to empower a far more diverse and inclusive group of citizens with the knowledge and skills to partake in the AI-economy and to apply these technologies to improve their lives and solve problems facing their communities. To achieve this vision, we are collaborating with industry, vocational-technical schools, and our teacher training programs to create curriculums, teacher-training materials, and assessments that keep pace with this rapidly evolving area. Our ultimate goal is to drive transformative impact in an ethical, inclusive and democratized way -- providing employment opportunities to close the prosperity & opportunity divide -- starting in Massachusetts, expanding across America’s Heartland and across the globe.



Classroom of the Future

There is tremendous opportunity to incorporate AI into the classroom in a personalized and emotionally compelling way that is effective, scalable and affordable. Such technologies should empower teachers and engage students while respecting privacy and being mindful of broader social implications. In particular, intelligent agents that serve as leaning companions, mentors, or practice partners have the potential to supplement and assist teachers – especially in much needed areas such as special needs education or for children who need additional support.
By creating new learning environments that support machine readable mentoring/learning interactions (between teachers and students as well as among student peers), we can generate new data sources about teaching and learning that will lead to transformative discoveries and innovation in AI in Education with the potential to even extend beyond the classroom to support learners at home, in the workplace, etc. There is also tremendous opportunity to develop AI tutors to support teacher training, especially for new topics such as K-12 and Vocational AI education, to augment and scale adult capacity building cost-effectively.



Creative and Empathetic Makers

Developing high-tech solutions to complex problems requires creativity, social intelligence, critical thinking, and working as part of a team with diverse perspectives and expertise. As algorithms automate more and more routine tasks and redefine job requirements, these human qualities become even more important as people work closely and collaboratively with intelligent machines and each other. Importantly, creative activities coupled with acting and designing with compassion towards others, bring meaning, purpose and joy to people’s lives. Our constructivist learning practices and innovative technologies are designed to engage students of all ages in creative thinking and expression, with opportunities to work on projects of personal meaning and passion, while in playful collaboration with peers. These principles --known as the Four P’s of Creative Learning: Projects, Passion, Peers and Play -- are embedded in all the materials, activities, and technologies we invent. Cultivating our creativity, empathy, and ethical judgment as the designers of tomorrow is not just an economic imperative, it is also critical for human fulfillment and opportunity for humans to be more human in a world of intelligent machines.

Meet the team behind Innovating Learning and Education in the Era of AI

People

We bring together diverse expertise from faculty, staff and students across all 5 schools of MIT

Cynthia Breazeal

Associate Professor, MIT Media Lab, Personal Robots Group Associate Director of Strategic Initiatives for the Bridge, MIT Quest for Intelligence


Breazeal leads this project with Sanjay Sarma to democratize AI and foster diversity and inclusivity. For this project, her research investigates new ways for K-12 students to learn about AI concepts, practices and ethics by designing, programming, training and interacting with robots, AI toolkits and other technologies. Her research also develops personalized learning companions for language, literacy and social-emotional development.

Sanjay Sarma

Professor, Department of Mechanical Engineering Vice President, Open Learning


Sarma is the VP for Open Learning at MIT, which includes the Office of Digital Learning, the MIT Integrated Learning Initiative and the Abdul Latif Jameel World Education Lab. His research developed new standards for RFID and he has been very influental in the field of Internet of Things -- that are becoming increasingy intelligent and connected. His core interest areas for this project are K-12 AI education, vocational-technical AI training, and the future workforce.

Eric Klopfer

Professor, Department of Urban Studies and Planning Director, Eduction Arcade Director, Scheller Teacher Education Program co-Director, J-WEL World Education Lab


Klopfer is an expert in serious game design to promote student engagement and learning in STEM. His work uses a design based research methodology to span the K-12 educational technology ecosystem, from design and development of new technologies to professional development and implementation to prepare the next geneation of STEM educators.


Mitchell Resnick

Professor, Media Lab, Lifelong Kindergarten Group Founder, The Scratch Foundation


Resnick is a world leader in constructionist appraoches to creative learning. His team develops the Scratch programming software and online community, used by over 200 million children and educators around the world. He is author of the book "Lifelong Kindergarten: Cultivating Creativity through Projects, Passion, Peers and Play." His core interest in this project is to develop new technologies and methods to foster creativity in the era of AI.  

Katherine Gallagher

AI Software Engineer, The Bridge, MIT Quest for Intelligence


Gallagher is an expert in machine learning and data science. She is actively developing customized tools for non-AI resarchers to help accelerate progress through the application of advanced computing. She is playing a key role the Bridge's effors to develop Jupityr notebooks to educate students and to faciliate the application of machine learning techniques and workflows to interesting problem domains and datasets. She is particulary active in developing Juiptyr notebooks for undergraduate and high school students for this project. 

Hal Abelson

Professor of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science, MIT CSAIL Founder, App Inventor


Abelson is a pioneer of constructionist learning and computational thinking. He has written numerous textbooks and developed high impact tools and programming languages to help students of all ages learn about AI and computer science. His group developed App Inventor that empowers people with little programming experience to write mobile phone applications. For this project, his team is expanding App Inventor to help students learn about AI and develop AI-enabled mobile apps.  


David Birnbach

Lecturer, MIT Sloan School of Management


Birnbach's research focus is at the intersection of technology innovation, entrepreneurship, and vocational education. He is a faculty mentor in several Action Learning programs including the Global Entrepreneurship (G-Lab), Enterprise Management (EM-Lab), and the China and India Lab. He helps students put classroom theory into practice, as students work side-by-side with organizations to apply classroom lessons to high-impact business and society challenges. For this project, his key area of focus is in creating a new AI career pathway for vocational-technical education.

Abby Everett Jaques

Postdoctoral Assoiate, Department of Philosophy Ethics Project Lead, MIT Quest for Intelligence


Jaques's research is centered in moral and political philosophy and the philosophy of action, and she is particularly interested in the relationship of ethics, AI and society. She is co-organizer of MIT's Computational Cultures initiative, and she is a Research Fellow in Digital Ethics at the Jain Family Institute. She leads several curriculum projects aimed at reinventing how we teach ethics to engineers. For this project, she has been extending these frameworks to K12 AI Ethics.  

George Westerman

Principal Research Scientist, MIT Sloan Initiative on the Digital Economy Faculty Director, J-WEL Workforce Learning Principal Research Scientist


Westerman's research and teaching focus on digital technology leadership and innovation. His work examines the transformative impact of digital innovation and AI on companies and workers, and understanding the changing demands for skills in today’s economy. He leads J-WEL’s efforts to transform the nature of workforce learning, and for this project his interests focus on the role and impact of AI in this area. 


John Gabrieli

Professor, Department of Brain and Cognitive Sciences Director, Martinos Imaging Center Associate Member, McGovern Institute for Brain Research


John Gabrieli is a neuroscientist at MIT, and he also has appointments at the Harvard Graduate School of Education and the Department of Psychiatry at Massachusetts General Hospital. His research focus is on the neural mechanisms of memory, cognition, nad emotion in the human brain -- and how those mechanisms are disrupted in neurological and psychiatric disorders. He has particular expertise in assessments for early litseracy skills and autism spectrum disorder that can inform the development of personalized AI interventions. For the purposes of this project, his core interest is in early assessment and intervention to support young learners with special needs.

Randi Williams

Graduate Student, MIT Media Lab, Personal Robots Group


Randi Williams is a graduate research assistant in the Personal Robots group at the MIT Media Lab. She received her Master of Science in Media Arts in Sciences from MIT in 2018 and her Bachelor of Science in Computer Engineering from UMBC in 2016. Randi has developed a number of AI education projects, including PopBots, social robot learning companions that teach AI through social interaction. Additionally, she works with her group to run studies that investigate children’s relationships with intelligent agents. To combat the current trend of misunderstanding and mistrust of AI, Randi is preparing the next generation to use AI to solve important problems in their communities.
