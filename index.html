A New MIT Project

Innovating Learning and Education in the era of AI

Artificial Intelligence is transforming and disrupting many industries and markets. Around the world, governments and companies are proclaiming we are entering the “era of AI” with the rapid development of intelligent devices that can recognize faces and interact via speech, robots that can work alongside people to help automate warehouse logistics and manufacture goods, algorithms that can generate novel photo realistic images and music, computers that can provide decision support to clinicians to help detect cancer more reliably, and so much more.

As computers continue to automate more and more routine tasks, inequity of education remains a key barrier to future opportunities where success depends increasingly on intellect, creativity, empathy, and having the right skills and knowledge. This accelerating change raises the critical question of how to best prepare students, from children to life-long learners, to be successful and to flourish in the era of AI.



AI Literacy in K-12

Just as computational thinking has become recognized as a new literacy for the 21st century, AI literacy is rapidly becoming recognized as important for STEM education on a global scale. We are actively working with schools, afterschool programs, education non-profits, and industry collaborators to develop the first comprehensive K-12 AI education program to serve students, teachers, and families nationally and worldwide. We believe that the best way to learn about AI-based concepts and practices is through hands-on projects supported by peers and mentors. We are extending student-friendly coding platforms (such as Scratch, App Inventor, and Jupyter Notebooks) with AI services, content, and curriculum to empower students to create personally and socially meaningful projects in a spirit of playful experimentation, creative expression, teamwork, critical thinking and problem solving. We are also developing teacher training materials, project guides, assessments and standards aligned with the AAAI-CTSA’s Big 5 Ideas of AI: machine perception, knowledge representation and decision making, machine learning, human-AI interaction and societal impact of AI. Though our innovative approach, students learn how intelligent machines think and learn through designing, programming, training, and interacting with them. We are also integrating ethical design concepts and practices so that students appreciate issues of bias, fairness, transparency, and privacy in what they create. MIT will also host expositions and competitions for K-12 students as a way to showcase their AI for Social Good projects to grow a vibrant community of young AI makers and innovators.



AI Career Pathway for Vocational-Technical Education.

In response to how AI is transforming the future of work, we aim to prepare students in vocational-technical training to learn, understand, and apply AI concepts and practices so they can engage in rewarding professional opportunities in the growing AI-economy. Our goal is to create a new AI Career Pathway for vocational-technical schools.
This pathway curriculum includes core AI literacy concepts, tools, and skills paired with Action Learning Labs to enable vocational-technical schools to offer a truly innovative approach to learning. In doing so, we aspire to empower a far more diverse and inclusive group of citizens with the knowledge and skills to partake in the AI-economy and to apply these technologies to improve their lives and solve problems facing their communities. We are collaborating with industry, vocational-technical schools, and our teacher training programs to create curriculums, teacher-training materials, and assessments that keep pace with this rapidly evolving area. We also see opportunity to develop AI tutors to amplify teacher training programs, especially for new topics such as K-12 and Vocational AI education, to scale adult capacity building cost-effectively. Our ultimate goal is to drive transformative impact in an ethical, inclusive and democratized way -- providing employment opportunities to close the prosperity & opportunity divide -- starting in Massachusetts and then expanding across America’s Heartland and around the globe.



Learning Companions for Special Needs and Support

There is tremendous opportunity to incorporate AI into the classroom in a personalized and emotionally compelling way that is effective, scalable and affordable. Such technologies should empower teachers and engage students while respecting privacy and being mindful of broader social implications. In particular, intelligent agents that serve as leaning companions, mentors, or practice partners have the potential to supplement and assist teachers – especially in much needed areas such as special needs education or for children who need additional support. We aspire to develop AI-enabled solutions to address problems that challenge our nation’s ability to provide high quality education to all children such as lack of access to affordable quality preschool programs, children who are English language learners, and increasing diagnosis rates of developmental disorders such ASD. We believe that developing a continuity of intervention that supports parents at home, the education team at school, and clinical team as needed, will yield significant positive results.



Learning Skills in the Era of AI

Developing high-tech solutions to complex problems requires creativity, social intelligence, critical thinking, and working as part of a team with diverse perspectives and expertise. As algorithms automate more and more routine tasks and redefine job requirements, these human qualities become even more important as people work closely and collaboratively with intelligent machines and each other. Importantly, creative activities coupled with acting and designing with compassion towards others, bring meaning, purpose and joy to people’s lives. Our constructivist learning practices and innovative technologies are designed to engage students of all ages in creative thinking and expression, with opportunities to work on projects of personal meaning and passion, while in playful collaboration with peers. These principles are known as the Four P’s of Creative Learning: Projects, Passion, Peers and Play. They are embedded in all the materials, activities, and technologies we invent whether they serve AI education or other topics and skills. Cultivating our creativity, empathy, and ethical judgment as the designers of tomorrow is not just an economic imperative, it is also critical for human fulfillment and opportunity for humans to be more human in a world of intelligent machines.

Cynthia Breazeal

Associate Professor, MIT Media Lab, Personal Robots Group

Associate Director of Strategic Initiatives for the Bridge, MIT Quest for Intelligence


Breazeal leads this project with Sanjay Sarma to democratize AI and foster diversity and inclusivity. For this project, her research investigates new ways for K-12 students to learn about AI concepts, practices and ethics by designing, programming, training and interacting with robots, AI toolkits and other technologies. Her research also develops personalized learning companions for language, literacy and social-emotional development.

Sanjay Sarma

Professor, Department of Mechanical Engineering

Vice President, MIT Open Learning Initiative


Sarma is the VP for Open Learning at MIT, which includes the Office of Digital Learning, the MIT Integrated Learning Initiative and the Abdul Latif Jameel World Education Lab. His research developed new standards for RFID and he has been very influental in the field of Internet of Things -- that are becoming increasingy intelligent and connected. His core interest areas for this project are K-12 AI education, vocational-technical AI training, and the future workforce.


Hal Abelson

Professor of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science, MIT CSAIL

Founder, App Inventor


Abelson is a pioneer of constructionist learning and computational thinking. He has written numerous textbooks and developed high impact tools and programming languages to help students of all ages learn about AI and computer science. His group developed App Inventor that empowers people with little programming experience to write mobile phone applications. For this project, his team is expanding App Inventor to help students learn about AI and develop AI-enabled mobile apps.

Katherine Gallagher

AI Software Engineer, The Bridge, MIT Quest for Intelligence


As an AI Software Engineer at the Bridge, Gallagher designs and constructs customized tools that make advances in machine learning accessible and intuitive to non-AI students and researchers. For this project, she is playing a key role in the Bridge's efforts to develop engaging educational applications with jupyter notebooks that allow students to explore machine learning concepts and techniques across interesting problem domains and datasets.

Mitchell Resnick

Professor, Media Lab, Lifelong Kindergarten Group

Founder, The Scratch Foundation


Resnick is a world leader in constructionist appraoches to creative learning. His team develops the Scratch programming software and online community, used by over 200 million children and educators around the world. He is author of the book "Lifelong Kindergarten: Cultivating Creativity through Projects, Passion, Peers and Play." His core interest in this project is to develop new technologies and methods to foster creativity in the era of AI.


Eric Klopfer

Professor, Department of Urban Studies and Planning

Director, Eduction Arcade

Director, Scheller Teacher Education Program


Klopfer is an expert in serious game design to promote student engagement and learning in STEM. His work uses a design based research methodology to span the K-12 educational technology ecosystem, from design and development of new technologies to professional development and implementation to prepare the next geneation of STEM educators. He is also co-director of the J-WEL World Eduation Lab.

Abby Everett Jaques

Postdoctoral Assoiate, Department of Philosophy

Ethics Project Lead, MIT Quest for Intelligence


Jaques's research is centered in moral and political philosophy and the philosophy of action, and she is particularly interested in the relationship of ethics, AI and society. She is co-organizer of MIT's Computational Cultures initiative, and she is a Research Fellow in Digital Ethics at the Jain Family Institute. She leads several curriculum projects aimed at reinventing how we teach ethics to engineers. For this project, she has been extending these frameworks to K12 AI Ethics.

John Gabrieli

Professor, Department of Brain and Cognitive Sciences

Director, Martinos Imaging Center


John Gabrieli is a neuroscientist and Associate Member of the McGovern Institute for Brain Research. He also has appointments at the Harvard Graduate School of Education and the Department of Psychiatry at Massachusetts General Hospital. His research focus is on the neural mechanisms of memory, cognition, nad emotion in the human brain -- and how those mechanisms are disrupted in neurological and psychiatric disorders. For the purposes of this project, his core interest is in early assessment and intervention to support young learners with special needs, esp. ASD.


David Birnbach

Lecturer, MIT Sloan School of Management


Birnbach's research focus is at the intersection of technology innovation, entrepreneurship, and vocational education. He is a faculty mentor in several Action Learning programs including the Global Entrepreneurship (G-Lab), Enterprise Management (EM-Lab), and the China and India Lab. He helps students put classroom theory into practice, as students work side-by-side with organizations to apply classroom lessons to high-impact business and society challenges. For this project, his key area of focus is in creating a new AI career pathway for vocational-technical education.

George Westerman

Senior Lecturer, MIT Sloan School of Management

Faculty Director, Workforce Learning, MIT Jameel World Education Lab


George works at the intersection of executive leadership and technology strategy. During his 16 years with MIT Sloan School of Management, he has written three award-winning books, including Leading Digital: Turning Technology Into Business Transformation. George has published papers in Harvard Business Review, Sloan Management Review, Organization Science, and other top journals. He is now focused on helping employers, educators, and other groups to rethink the process of workforce learning around the world. George is co-chair of the MIT Sloan CIO Leadership Awards, faculty advisor to the Board of Directors for the Technology Business Management Council, and a member of the Digital Strategy Roundtable for the US Library of Congress.

Randi Williams

Graduate Student, MIT Media Lab, Personal Robots Group


Randi Williams is a graduate research assistant in the Personal Robots group at the MIT Media Lab. She received her Master of Science in Media Arts in Sciences from MIT in 2018 and her Bachelor of Science in Computer Engineering from UMBC in 2016. Randi has developed a number of AI education projects, including PopBots, social robot learning companions that teach AI through social interaction. Additionally, she works with her group to run studies that investigate children’s relationships with intelligent agents. To combat the current trend of misunderstanding and mistrust of AI, Randi is preparing the next generation to use AI to solve important problems in their communities.


Safinah Ali

Graduate Student, MIT Media Lab, Personal Robots Group


Safinah is a graduate research assistant in the Personal Robots Group at MIT Media Lab. Safinah's work focuses on leveraging Child-Robot Interaction to enhance early childhood literacy as well as develop children's learning mindsets such as creativity, growth mindset and curiosity. Her recent work involves designing game interactions that afford children and embodied robots co-create art. Safinah's research focus is to make AI assisted learning tools accessible to all in the form of delightful game-like experiences. Her previous work has revolved around educational game design, assistive technology and HCI.

Huili Chen

Graduate Student, MIT Media Lab, Personal Robots Group


Huili Chen is a graduate research assistant in the Personal Robots group at the MIT Media Lab. She received her M.S. in Media Arts in Sciences from MIT in 2018, and her B.S. in Computer Science and B.A. in Psychology from Notre Dame in 2016. Huili’s research interest encompasses computing, human-computer interaction and social science. One of her past projects is to study how a robot should switch its social roles to facilitate children’s learning. Now she is using machine learning to discover and model interaction dynamics between parent and child.

Samuel Spalding

Graduate Student, MIT Media Lab, Personal Robots Group


Sam Spaulding is a doctoral student with the Personal Robots Group at the MIT Media Lab. His thesis research focuses on developing the underlying technology for social robot learning companions to support early-language and literacy skills, synthesizing insights from machine learning, affective and educational sciences, and children’s media design. His research has been presented at numerous international conferences and has been recognized by an NSF Graduate Research Fellowship, a Human-Robot Interaction Pioneers award, and research grants from the Mellon Fund and Sigma Xi scientific society.


Jim Gray

Research Scientist, MIT Media Lab, Personal Robots Group


Jim Gray is a Research Scientist with the Personal Robots Group, where he contributes expertise in children’s learning, development, play, media design, innovative learning technologies, and product development. With a career bridging academia and business, he provides product management for K-12 educational deployment projects; curriculum design; strategic planning and grant writing; along with guidance, design, mentoring, and support on a variety of learning and child development related projects — particularly as they pertain to technology and early childhood.

Andrew Sliwinski

Research Scientist, MIT Media Lab, Lifelong Kindergarten


Andrew Sliwinski is a research scientist with the Lifelong Kindergarten group at the MIT Media Lab. His work focuses on the intersection between learning, creativity, and technology. Andrew serves as co-director of Scratch – an online community and tool for kids to explore their creativity through computing which reaches over two hundred million children every year. Prior to joining MIT, Andrew co-founded the learning organization DIY Co. which was acquired by LittleBits in 2018.

Blakely H Payne

Blakeley H. Payne is a graduate research assistant at the MIT Media Lab where she studies the ethics of artificial intelligence. Specifically, she develops educational materials to teach children to be both conscientious consumers and designers of AI systems. Before joining MIT, she completed her undergraduate studies at the University of South Carolina, where she earned a B.S. in Computer Science and Mathematics. Blakeley has completed several AI internships at institutions such as Adobe, Inc. and U.C. Berkeley. Blakeley tweets, perhaps too often, @blakeleyhpayne.

Hae Won Park

Research Scientist, MIT Media Lab, Personal Robots Group


Hae Won Park is a Research Scientist at MIT Media Lab and a Principal Investigator of the Social Robot Companions Program. Her research focuses on socio-emotive AI and personalization of social robots that support long-term interaction and relationship between users and their robot companions. Her work spans a range of applications including education for young children and wellbeing benefits for older adults. Her research has been published at top robotics and AI venues and has received awards for best paper (HRI 2017), innovative robot applications (ICRA 2013), and pecha-kucha presentation (ICRA 2014). Hae Won received her PhD from Georgia Tech in 2014, at which time she also co-founded Zyrobotics, an assistive education robotics startup that was recognized as the best 2015 US robotics startup by Robohub and was the finalist of the Intel Innovation Award.
